A sea is a large body of salty water. There are particular seas and the sea. The sea commonly refers to the ocean, the wider body of seawater. Particular seas are either marginal seas, second-order sections of the oceanic sea (e.g. the Mediterranean Sea), or certain large, nearly landlocked bodies of water.

The ocean is a vast body of saltwater that covers approximately 71 percent of Earth’s surface. Oceanographers and countries have traditionally divided it into four distinct regions: the Pacific, Atlantic, Indian, and Arctic oceans. In the 20th century, some oceanographers labeled the seas around Antarctica as the Southern Ocean, which was officially recognized by National Geographic in 2021 as the fifth ocean1. The ocean plays a crucial role in shaping our planet—it impacts weather patterns, temperature regulation, and the global food supply. Despite its significance, more than 80 percent of the ocean remains unexplored and unmapped. The ocean floor contains towering mountain ranges, deep canyons (trenches), and fascinating ecosystems. For instance, the peak of Mount Everest—the world’s tallest mountain—would barely break the ocean’s surface if placed in the Mariana Trench or Philippine Trench, two of the deepest parts of the ocean. With countless species still undiscovered, oceanographers estimate that there may be anywhere from a few hundred thousand to several million more species awaiting exploration. Currently, scientists know of around 226,000 ocean species, but the mysteries of the deep continue to captivate our curiosity
